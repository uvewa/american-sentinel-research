1. Is religious liberty a civil right or a religious right?

2. Is it the right to worship, profoundly, or publicly, worship to the dictate of one's own conscience?

3. Is also the right not to worship?

4. Of whose conscience is this the right of worship or of not worship?

5. Is it the right of a heathen commissioner? If not, why not?

6. Is this right of conscience subject to the whims of popular majorities?

7. Has civil government any rightful control of conscience and belief?

8. Has government the right to prescribe any religious duty to its citizens? [page 188]

9. Has it the right to appoint solemn days of fasting and prayer?

10. Has the President of the United States, in his official capacity, the right to appoint days of thanksgiving? If so, why?

11. Has our government the right to favor Mohammedanism to Buddhism?

12. Has it the right to favor any religion, denomination, or number thereof?

13. Has it the right to extend special favors to Christianity?

14. Has it the right to furnish salaried positions to preachers, who cannot find rich employment otherwise, simply because they are preachers?

15. Has it the right to pay for religious instruction for its soldiers, criminals, schools, or legislators?

16. Has it a right to exempt church property from taxation?

17. Has it the right to exempt any individual from the penalty of violating law because of his peculiar religious convictions?

18. Ought Sunday laws to exempt any person for conscientious reasons?

19. Has government the right to pass any law, which, when applied to all without exemption, would infringe any one's civil rights?

20. Has government the right to punish any person, or deprive any of the fruits of his time and labor, except for crime?

21. Any practice—religious or secular—which does not interfere with the equal rights of all really a crime?

22. Has government the right to make such a position criminal?

23. Has it the right to prohibit polygamy? Why?

24. Has government the right to prohibit immorality and the worship of images, or of the images of saints?

25. Has it the right to prohibit idolatry?

26. Has it the right to prohibit the public condemnation of crude practices, when this is not done in the special places of such worship?

27. May it rightfully punish any person for denying the existence of a supreme being?

28. Has it the right to punish blasphemy?

29. Has it the right to punish any person for quietly observing one day in seven as the Sabbath?

30. Has it the right to compel or to command him to observe it as such?

31. May it rightfully punish him for not observing it?

32. Has it a right to require total rest of citizens in private life on legal holidays?

33. Has it the right to require any such person to cease from his ordinary labor on any day of the week—on any day?

34. Has it the right to pass laws requiring such a person to rest from his ordinary labor on Sunday, either as a holiday or sacred day?

35. Has government the right to prohibit disturbance of public meetings for [page 189] worship or secular purposes?—On all days?

36. Will a certain act or form of labor disturb worship in churches more on Wednesdays than on Sunday?

37. Under the same conditions will it produce more noise on Sundays than on other days?

38. What is responsible for changed conditions on Sunday, if any?

39. Are religious people more liable to be disturbed on Sunday than on other days? If so, why?

40. Has any government the right to pass labor laws which one person may properly consider as shielding for establishing his distinctive religious sentiments?

41. Can government take cognizance of any personal religious opinions as a rightful basis of its proceedings of another?

42. Did Christ teach his followers to persecute or present measures in the interest of his religion?

43. Is an innocent thought or act more immoral if it occurs on a particular day of the week? A crime more criminal?

44. May an act which does not disturb church worship be a crime on one day of the week but not on other days?

45. Would such an act of disturbance be a crime because it disturbed the public peace or because it occurred on a designated day?

46. Does the criminality of an act depend on the day of the week when it is done?

47. In principle, is the "Sunday newspaper" worse than the Wednesday edition? Does Saturday batter dough Sunday milk?

48. Is it morally or civilly wrong for daily newspapers to publish on Saturday and Monday matter which ought not be published or read on Sunday?

49. Is it wrong for the same matter to appear in the Sunday newspaper itself?

50. Who is technically more responsible for the circulation of the Sunday paper than is the pressman, compositor or editor who sets it?

51. Of whom is this technically personal?

52. Do Christians read them? If so, why?

53. If Christians do not read them, are they responsible for what others choose to read on Thursday or Sunday?

54. Can government punish a man for the mental state of covetousness?

55. Can government properly legislate on any subject matter of the last commandment of the decalogue?

56. Can government punish every word and act of disrespect to parents, or every manifestation of one's neighbor?

57. Then can it rightfully attempt by legislation to reach all offenses covered by the fifth and ninth commandments of the Decalogue?

58. May it unquestionably legislate against murder, adultery, and theft? Why?

59. Can civil government compel a person to love his neighbor as himself?

60. Can it, then, legislate on the subject matter of the last six commandments as such?

61. Can it rightfully legislate at all on the first four commandments? Are the duties therein prescribed duties to man or to God?

62. Does civil government rightfully legislate against certain crimes because they are offenses against some of the last six commandments, or because they are a violation of natural rights?

63. Does it then legislate because of those commandments, or to protect the rights and property of men?

64. Can it, then, presume to legislate for God, in matters which do not concern each of man's relations to another or to his own possessions?

65. How positively is this distinction between man's duties to God and those to man made in the teaching of Jesus Christ? Matt. 22:21.

66. Is there any other distinction or principle that precisely marks off both individual rights of conscience, of religion, the rights and duties of civil government?

67. Does our national Constitution and the Declaration of Independence contain these principles of the civil and religious rights?

68. By whom will those rights be endangered, if ever? By the atheist? By the Mormon?

69. Can any reform of public morality or religious civilization justify civil legislation on the subject matter of the first four commandments of the Decalogue?

70. Have our national or State governments ever invaded this sacred territory? Is it the tendency to withdraw from or, to encroach still further?

72. What is the duty of every true American? FRANK WILLIAM HOWE. *Government Building.*
