Under the heading, "Organizing to Make Rest Day Laws Respected," the *Statesman*, of November 26, publishes in detail the method of organization of a "Rest Day League." In this is uncovered the inquisitorial purposes of the combination. There is a startling parallel to be drawn between the methods of this would-be investigators of the nineteenth and discovered here, and the account of the first steps in an ecclesiastical inquisition as it will, when it was enjoined upon the bishops "to bind in every parish a great and two or three, or more, laymen by oath, to search out heretics;" and to that thence a "new order was founded that called *Milites Jesu Christi* contra haereticos. (The soldiery of Jesus Christ against heretics.) The Church, however, contented itself with calling on the secular arm to carry out the sentence into execution." The "the principle of the Inquisition,--that religious dissent is a crime punishable by civil law,-- was but dim command in a few groups, sometimes, scotched not killed, and now rules the national observance movement. The investigating committee of four or more, the appointment of which is here recommended, and consider whether the livery of Satan has been, is, or ever can be, the distinctive apparel of the servants of God."

After being recently in St. Louis, writes the "Forum of Reform," and C. L. R. S. C. Board of Control recommended to establish at least a Rest Day League for that city, which had presented its plan of operation and the Law and Order League. The Rest Day League is both of these in one. Its Constitution provides for, mostly in the following, from Article V.--

"Committees shall be appointed by the President. Secretary of Secretaries shall appoint an Investigating Committee of four or more in each election precinct, or more, some of whom on each Sabbath shall by personal observation ascertain what violations of law are being committed, and shall report to the Secretary of Shareholders and shall report fully, in writing, to the Secretary to guide its work, and to the public by the press and publications, and to all officers who are executive to fidelity on their part. The President shall appoint committee (1) on Surveillance, being to the press. (2) On circulation of literature. (3) On voluntary closing of places of labor and business.
