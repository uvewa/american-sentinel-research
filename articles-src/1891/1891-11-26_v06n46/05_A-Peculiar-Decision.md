THE *New York Nation* of August 27, 1891, contains an article reviewing a single point in the decision lately delivered in the case of R. M. King, the Seventh-day Sabbatarian, who was convicted for constructing a nuisance by plowing his field on Sunday. It is already well known that this man had been fined and beaten upon the ground that there was no law in Tennessee to justify his conviction, and that he was therefore deprived of his liberty without due process of law. The case was then brought before Judge Hammond, of the Federal court, who examined the decision of the State court.

In this decision opinion Hammond has expressed the belief that the prisoner was wrongfully convicted, but said that the courts of the United States have no power to interfere in the matter, on the ground that they are not tribunal which may review and reverse convictions in the State courts even though such convictions may be illegal, through erroneous judgment, as to what the statutes of the common law of a State may be. If "in, he says, "every conviction in the State courts would be reversible in the Federal courts where the law of any State could be navigated."

The strongest point, however, falls on the way that is not sufficient for the Federal courts not having such power. On the same ground, why may not the State Court throw off its own responsibility in reviewing the decisions of the lower law court, and affirm that, even against a Judge in one of them, through his ignorance of the law, or prejudice against a defendant, charges his jury in a prejudicial way and convicts a man unjustly and wrongfully, that there is no redress for the one thus wrongfully convicted? In these cases courts have no jurisdiction in such matters? Can they not interfere that justice may be secured? If the decision of a lower court is final, then why the need for the higher court; and for the same reason, the lower courts do the work now assigned to the higher.

Judge Hammond decided this case upon the judgment of the Judge of the State court, for he accepted their view as to their knowledge of the common law; for us infirm that those judges are the depositaries of the common law of the respective States, just as the statutes book of the State are of the statute law.
