## Discussion at the Sunset Club.

THE Sunset Club, of Chicago, an organization of professional, business, and literary men, which dines every Thursday evening at the Grand Pacific Hotel, and regularly serves at its banquets a feast course of wit, reason, and eloquence, took up the subject for deliberation in its meeting of Thursday, October 22, the question of "Sunday at the World's Fair." The *Tribune* Professor reports Judge McDonald as presenting substantially the following views:

The principal difficulty which has embarrassed me in the consideration of this question has been to divest myself of certain personal views. I thoroughly recognize them because I do certainly think on Sunday it should be our main unsearchable duty, that everybody else ought to do the same. Or that, if I want to attend the Exposition on that day, no other, by its nature, ought not in any manner to influence me in determining what I would do to serve as at the proper place for the managers of the Exposition to adopt.

I am inclined to look at the whole matter from a practical point of view, and I trust the Exposition managers will consider it in no other way.

I ask that the Exposition be open on Sunday, for the benefit of all, but principally for those tired men who work eight and ten hours of every week-day, and who, with the slender margin of savings that always stands between them and want, may not have an opportunity besides any working day for recreation or instruction. Of all our citizens, they need to see this great exhibition of the results of labor. Many men working in the narrow lines, in the smoke and turmoil and noise of the factories, are seldom enabled to see the achievements of united labor. They work and sweat, and sometimes wonder for what, perhaps feeling that merely to live and eat so much every day is not much of an achievement. They fail to appreciate their relations and their usefulness to the rest of the world. But here, on this one Sunday, for such it shall be, the workman shall come, with his wife and children, and he shall feel great and shall feel more than they know what he, with all other men, can create. Perhaps, too, he can get there, better than from any religion which he does not go to hear, the high philosophy that shall reconcile him to the necessity of struggle and him and his life.
