It was a noble characteristic of our statesmen that they regarded all men as equal, social, or, in other words, they recognized all persons in whatever sect or faith as equally entitled to carry out their religious views. It was not the spirit of the modern Sunday-law agitator who would drive Christians to keep the day of his choice sacred and compel all others to do so too; but it was freedom for the Christian, freedom for the Jew, freedom for the pagan, and freedom for the agnostic. Madison, in a different frame, heard and considering, assigns himself to reference to the law as "perfect equality" with all Christian sects, and in the following letter he makes his admiration in the plan that such a course is the surest way of securing ultimate harmony. He writes to a Jewish Rabbi, changing as follows:

Having ever regarded the freedom of religious opinions and worship as equally the right of every sect, and the secure enjoyment of it as the best human provision for bringing all either into the same way of truth, or into the mutual charity which is the only substitute, I observe with pleasure the view you give of the spirit in which your sect partake of the civil and political rights of the (the Jewish people) partake of the blessings of your Government.
