The new Spanish labor bill provides that all contracts for labor must be made not to contain prohibitions of work but not only on Sundays, but on all feast days as well. This makes about ninety holidays a year. The bill provides penalties for employers and employees alike for violations of the law. Exemptions are however provided for persons declaring that they do not belong to the State religion, as the framers must have been studying the Blair Sunday Rest Bill. Their provisions are quite similar.

---

Chief Justice Chase once said, "The wicked man are set in the penitentiaries; they are in the churches." And indeed it does seem so when we stop to consider the spirit manifested by some of the leading forms of the so-called worthy moral and religious men of the day. With National Reformers and Woman's Christian Temperance Union speakers, and American Sabbath Union representatives everywhere going out and threatening bloodshed if their demands are not speedily crystalized into law, it does seem that the modern Church has apostatized from Christ, and has become, as foretold in Isa. 14:17, "the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and the cage of every unclean and hateful bird." Certain it is that the spirit of National Reform and American Sabbathism Union is the very opposite of the spirit of Jesus Christ, for it is the spirit of force while his is the spirit of love and persuasion.

The *Christian Advocate,* of this city, in common with most of the religious press, expresses great indignation at the persecution of R. M. King, by most of the injustice Sunday law of Tennessee. But instead of coming out boldly and denouncing the outrages, the *Advocate* says: King, a Seventh-day Adventist, unmistakable played on Sunday, in Tennessee. He was arrested.

This is unworthy of the *Advocate*. The fact is that King's only work was done as quietly and as privately as it was possible to do it, and all the circumstances surrounding the case show that it was a case of religious persecution pure and simple. King's offense was not that he plowed on Sunday, but that he did not plow or do any work on Saturday. She [page 256] moves other persons in the same neighborhood have always worked on Sunday when they chose so to do, and nothing has ever been said or done about it; nor was King molested until he commenced to observe another day.
