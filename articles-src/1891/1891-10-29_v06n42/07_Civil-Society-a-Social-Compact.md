It is the organized . . . based on the civil, as well as a moral and intellectual impulse him toward political society. He enters his compact for . . . purposes which contain not only moral acts, but depend, for their highest development, upon association with the believer, and with this the social requirements is not justified and at certain points defined advantages are exclusively set forth for Christian instruction, and with these provisions he expects, and he attains the highest efficiency for the promulgation and the development of the individual relations of each other.

There is a sense, indeed, in which the Christian may view the civil government with interest and advantage to exchange in those advantages which accrue to him because a citizen in compact with his fellows. But this advantage comes from the compact; and such concession can not only secure a gain, but sacrifice the Christian principle, if its highest advantage, that of Christian liberty, is forfeited.

The obligation of a society, then, is in the direction of the highest development of its individual members; and the methods of civilization progress are not toward the incorporation of civil power, but toward the better establishing and perfecting of it.

Only in this freely associated character of the civil society is a social compact between men setting a free, full social and moral, beings, and so far as they are Christians so be they.
