The State has nothing whatever to do with enforcing and administering the moral law. Man must answer for himself to God; which would not be the case if God, which was entrusted to some between the individual and the. In this the State, and the State and State, and the State to God; there would be is that one case so direct accountability to God; the citizens would inquire, what does the State say, and knowing and doing the will of the State, the individual would feel safe. But such is not the plan of God. He has not ordained civil governments to regulate the civil affairs of man, and it is in civil affairs that man deals with his fellow man; but in morals, man are morewise in accountability to God. He is the one moral governor, and his law is the one moral law.

The State can not make laws touching religion on the plan of its own responsibility; and the citizens do not need such laws, because each one is personally responsible to another tribunal infinitely higher than the State, for his standing as morals. Then all that the State can do, is to take from people any man of a set of men, from interfering others in the enjoyment of their peculiar form of worship.
