# The Establishment of Religion by Colonial Grant and Charter.

In pursuance of the policy of establishing the Christian religion in Plymouth Colony and the other colonies settled by the Puritans and other colonists, and in addition to the laws to "punish neglecters of the worship of God, and Sabbath breakers awarer or curse;" those who "neglect the frequenting of the public worship of God;" the stern decrees against "the heathen" for those who "ran away from their master, or plant, or carry burdens on the Lord's day, or "disorderly tippling and any spiritling or corruption, the corporal punishment, at the discretion of the magistrate, of such as deny the Scriptures to be the rule of life. The declaring against those who did not furnish the community according to their stations, that it should be in the power of the magistrate to use compulsory measures for pressing upon these duties;" those seem already to have been quite a sufficient foundation upon which to build an establishment of religion. But in every colony and court of court, however little the gospel may be perverted by law into a scheme for establishing religion goes on. In 1607, June 6, it was decreed:—

WHEREAS The General Court taking into their serious consideration the necessity of the establishment of religion, and the propagation of the gospel for want of an able, godly Teaching Ministry, that the precious souls in this wilderness may not like sheep without a shepherd, be lost; and being desirous something may be done therefor,— It is ordered and agreed by this court, that every inhabitant in any town through this jurisdiction shall be, inspired to as good a works of the God as God may; and in case any person shall neglect or refuse the public worship and due attendance thereupon, they shall forfeit and pay; fines and sums were then established therefor.

> It is ordered and agreed by this Court, that where any town by voluntary contribution or otherwise shall not provide for the public worship and the service of God there, the county court shall assess and order what a competent and suitable maintenance; and therefore order and agree. That what any person willfully and obstinately neglects to contribute to the support of such minister, may by the county court be assessed to the payment of such sum or sums as the law then or may direct.

Long before, this abominable condition of things had evoked the indignant protest from the poet Cowper:—

> But thy statutes wrenched from its design
> And made the models of sowing grace
> The foolish bond of passive obedience
> That inhabit may make their title good
> Whose only claim is that of force, oppressors—

Such were the results of making religion a matter of civil contract. *—Harrison Hall, England.*

> If we pass beyond these matters to a view of American life as expressed by its laws, its business, its customs and its society, we find everywhere a clear recognition of the same truth.
