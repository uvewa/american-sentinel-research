## Is It the Sabbath?

As THE SENTINEL has repeatedly stated, the most indubitable argument against the State has no right to decide religious questions, for to pass upon the truth or falsity of religious doctrines. It follows that the State has no right to examine the claims of Sunday, or of any other day, and decide that either that it is, or that it is not, the Sabbath. Inasmuch, however, as State recognition of Sunday as Sabbath necessitates its demanded on the ground that it is the Sabbath, and as that involves the law of God, and binding alike upon nations and individuals, the question, "Is it the Sabbath?" becomes a pertinent and interesting subject of personal inquiry, though, as before intimated, the merits of the examination can not affect in any way the right of the State to legislate upon the question. The State has none no such right.

Having made this proposition, we call to mind, for the reader's attention is invited to an examination of the claim that Sunday is the Sabbath.

That Sunday is by many regarded as the Sabbath, and that it is actually kept by some as such, does not affect the question. Is Sunday the Sabbath in a Bible sense, or by divine authority? This is the claim made by Sunday-law advocates. The claim that its observance shall be enforced by civil law. What then say the Scriptures of truth on this subject?

The first mention of the Sabbath in the Bible is in Genesis 2:1-3, where we learn that the seventh day God rested the work which he had made, and sanctified it, and as a day of rest from his work. This is the Sabbath day from all his work which he had made.

God blessed it because that in it he had rested from all his work which God created and made; and God blessed the seventh day and sanctified it; because that in it he had rested from all his work which God created and made. It is clear here that in is no command to keep the Sabbath, neither indeed is it called by that name; but we were told Gen. 2:3, it was sanctified, which means set apart for a holy use; and in Mark 2:27 we have the assurance of the Saviour that "the Sabbath was made for man." It seems reasonable, that at the very dawn of human history God gave man his holy day. But be that as it may, there is in Ex. 20:8-11 a positive command given: "Remember the Sabbath day to keep it holy," and it is explicitly stated: "the seventh day is the Sabbath." "For in six days the Lord made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that in them is, and he rested the seventh day: wherefore the Lord blessed the Sabbath day and hallowed it."

This identifies the Sabbath of the fourth commandment with the seventh day of creation week, not with a seventh day after six days of work, but with the seventh day, the memorial of the finished creation.

This is very frequently pointed out to the children of Israel before the giving of the law by the absence of manna upon it, and by the fact that that gathered upon the sixth day would keep over the Sabbath, while if kept over to any other day, what was left of it could not be kept in any other time. The record is thus briefly given in Ex. 16:23-30:

From Josh. 5:12 we know that the manna continued until they had entered the land of Canaan, forty years after they left Egypt. Thus fifty-two times every year for forty years, or a total of 7,800 times, was the identity of the seventh day, the Sabbath, pointed out to the children of Israel by the direct method of the manna keeping over the seventh day and none falling upon the seventh day. And this was not a man that the fourth commandment required be kept as of God to be kept, assuredly, it is a good question; and that day of rest was kept at the time of the crucifixion, not only by the Jews, but by the followers of Christ also, is expressly stated in Luke 23:56, and it was these women or of certain women that "they returned and prepared spices and ointments; and rested the Sabbath day according to the commandment." Now up to the end of the week, or very early in the morning, they came unto the sepulchre, bringing the spices which they had prepared.

From the text just quoted we learn two things; first, that at the time of the crucifixion of our Lord, the fourth commandment pointed to the observance of the Sabbath; for the women kept it "according to the commandment;" and second, that that day was the day just preceding the first day of the week, which must necessarily have been the seventh day of the week. Furthermore, it is very evident that the fourth commandment requires, not the observance of a seventh day, but of a very particular day after six days of labor; the seventh day of the week.

This is the week as created and well recognized through all history, as having its divisions of time, and that consequently the seventh day of the week is likewise a well defined period of time, is attested to all history. Moreover, the week in all ages runs on manipulable witnesses in all parts of the Monroe account, and it entails an impassable monument to the original Sabbath, the seventh day of the week.

Aside from the Bible, the strongest evidence, also, as to the origin of the week is its universality. Some have thought that it might have been suggested by the four changes of the moon, but it is sufficiently disproved by the theory that the moon does not change once in seven days and it even its days varying from each other considerably as may be. There is nothing extrinsingly in with the week itself beyond its coincidence with the period of creation, nothing in nature to suggest an septenary cycle, and it is practically beyond dispute, as the *Encyclopaedia Britannica*, article "Calendar" states.

As the week forms neither an aliquot part nor a major part of the lunar month, its use, it will be noticed will not be a loss in civil or religious life in the Roman Empire, without a trace of beginning or possibility.
