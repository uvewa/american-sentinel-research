# Some Scraps of New England History

SOME SUFFERING OF THE QUAKERS

In July, 1656, Mary Fisher and Anne Austin, two Quaker women, arrived in Boston. By some means, news of their coming had preceded them. Before they were suffered to come on shore from the vessel, the deputy-governor, Governor Bellingham, sent officers to search them. He said, "searched the trunks and chests, and took away their books, then detained, and carried them ashore, after awhile kept prisoners ashore; and the said books kept prisoners ashore; and the said books were, by the warrant of the above-said deputy, sent to be burned. The women were kept prisoners ashore; and the said books were, by the warrant of the magistrates, made the market-place by the hangman. The women were then stripped naked, however, and once 'what' on plea to prison; their persons were strictly searched for tokens of witchcraft. Having been kept five weeks in prison, during which time no person was allowed to come to them, not permitted to have any candles, they were sent away."

They were not provided with victuals, except by purchase. Nicholas Upsal, one who lived in Boston, and was a member of the church there, bought of the jailer, for five shillings a week, the privileges of furnishing them with food. . . . Another order was issued to the jailer, commanding him to search or allow to be seen, no more boxes, chests, and things of the Quakers furnishing and bringing to the custody, for pen, ink, papers and books, and to take care those from them.
