# How a Friday Holiday Became a First-Day Sabbath

How can Sunday be such a day as is claimed by those who observe it? In the Bible, when those very Scriptures above were written, there were no particular passages of Scripture showing any such change. The material for examination could be multiplied, if need be, and the difficulty of getting information from the biblical to the historical conditions of the centuries following into the hands of theologians is a very fruitful inquiry. The history of Sunday is a fiddle on which any tune can be played.

But to the question. Sunday was named by any biblical writer nor by any writer before the fourth century as a Sabbath or holy day. It was simply the day after the Sabbath. It was made sacred, using that word in a restricted sense, by the resurrection. There is no historical or scriptural statement of this change.

It was on Friday of March 15, A.D. 29, in the city of Jerusalem, on the cross of the crucifixion, that "he bowed his head" and died, commanding there be no disorder, and pronouncing "It is finished" upon the cross. This edict continued as a holiday to the Jewish community from that era, comprising the largest portion to this day of Israel that were promised. And that day was the day of preparation, the last day of the week. It was the day before the Sabbath. And it continued to be observed by Christian communities as such by some. Being already in the habit of keeping Friday, the Christians who were obliged to abstain from ordinary secular employment and from certain food, on the Sabbath or sacred day, observed with all the strictness of a Sabbath, the weekly religious day of Sunday.
