Ideal government has for its end the protection of individual rights, and the prevention of national welfare. The legitimate attainments of civil law can never be the morality of a nation, nor of the individual; for morality is purely a personal matter, and the civil law has nothing to do with the individual as such, but only as he is associated with others. It has no authority over a man’s motives, nor for an attempt on his own life, but only as he is found affecting his criminal intentions on another. The moral law lies far further back, recognizing the motive which has inspired the crime, and that alone.

Again, the civil law can not be said even to enforce good citizenship, except as far as good citizenship is coincident with justice. It is not a good citizenship for one man to defraud another in a cancelled bargain, and yet the civil law demands the fulfillment of that bargain. In the same way it insures fidelity to an honest execution. Another fact which has an important bearing on the relation which the moral law should sustain to civil government is, that the civil law is not, nor can it be, a prophylactic agency;—in other words, it has nothing to do with the criminal until his crime either has been actually committed or is believed to have been. The reason is obvious. It is manifestly unjust to argue that because malice in one person has led to crime, it shall be assumed in all; or if one man robs, we can always argue from what a man believes, to the good or evil in which that belief will terminate. We say that the practice of polygamy is injurious to the best interests of the State. So it is; but then is the point where civil law can deal with the evil. The fact that a man is a Mormon, and that Mormons have always practiced polygamy, is not sufficient. Every Mormon should be considered criminal in the eyes of the civil law.

It was charged that the Russian Jews, a few years ago, were injurious to the best interests of the Russian State; that they were a nation of usurers who exacted upon corrupted peasants, and that in a hundred other unlawful customs, and especially in their refusal to become good citizens of Russia; and the purpose of their expulsion from the Russian Empire as no less than religious persecution, became, no matter how many crimes may have been committed by Hebrew individuals, it does not follow that all who profess their creed would have committed the same or like offenses. To the neglect of this obvious truth may be traced all the great religious persecutions of history; for although the government finds its province only in meting out punishment for the actual offense, yet when it has fulfilled its mission the civil law has done its work.

Whether the man who has the loaf from the baker’s window is a hardened criminal of lifelong experience or the fortunate father of a starving child, there can be no discrimination in the eyes of the civil law. There may be a moral distinction between the man who kills his neighbour in cold blood in hope of gain, and the one who desires by that act to relieve society of a dangerous fellow, and yet if there is such a distinction, civil law has nothing to find in it, and justly so. It can not look at the motives which act behind the crime, except when they are evidenced so as to warrant escape; and, upon these rights, legislative bodies shall possess that wisdom, whereby, with the Almighty, they can read and judge the consciences of men; never, with all an infinite foresight, looking into an unknown future, they can predict unfailingly to what results the different temperaments of men, amid their respective environments will lead them, can they then assay the estimate of guilt from the injury itself, and not the principles of morality, convict the criminal before he has committed his crime.

There are two points of view from which we naturally regard a criminal offense,—the evil with which we associate it, and the personal injury which follows as its result. Obviously the former constitutes the sin of the action, and the latter we call the crime. In other words, the motion which would influence one to an evil offense, and the outreach and extent of which that notion, right or wrong, will lend the individual to act on two very different matters, which from a legislative standpoint by no means correspond to the respective provinces of Divine and human wisdom. To illustrate this, take the case of a familiar illustration, the storekeeper. The simplest observer is able to judge the thefts or defrauds or deficits of the pockets which upon the streets. With a man that is a man’s harness it will deter in themselves to recognize a perfect representation. But unless a man shall always be a competent judge of the conditions under which the negative will produce a perfect or definitive pattern, to tell what would be obtained by the relative light and shade, or the distance of the negative from the lens through which the representation is to be cast. Then between the thoughts and visible acts of man the lens of his proper environment lies, and it must deal with every individual. The notice which in one case would result in a praiseworthy action, might under other circumstances, know only to the individual and his Maker, lead to a criminal offense; and when man presumes to regulate his great code to condemn the actual and alike of his actions will find its terminus in notices of intolerance and religious persecution.

The moral law defines the future of men in God. It regards the offense from the standpoint of the transgressor, and not from that of the one against whom the wrong has been committed. In either case, it is everywhere, consistent alike of the general injustice and principle of religion; it generates a vengeance for holy truth; nor does it hesitate to destroy any pretence of humanity. In all this the civil power has no concern.
