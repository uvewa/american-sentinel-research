[*Paper read before the Methodist ministers' Monday Meeting, of Hoboken, N. J., by Rev. H. H. Brandt.*]

I REMARK this line of thought because I believe that there is a special danger at this day that men will lose faith in the gospel as the means of saving the world, at least from certain glaring sins, and look to politics to accomplish the results. The danger is that politics will be permitted to the first place, and the gospel is used of

all, retired to a subordinate place. There is no doubt that there is a tendency abroad in these days to substitute political machinery as a means of moral reform; to resort, in short, to the political machine expert, for the services of the church.

Now religion and politics are very closely related in the United States. Each pursues an independent course in its own sphere. The religious life having to do with spiritual things, has free course over all that pertains to the kingdom of heaven. The civil power, having to do with social affairs, controls in all the functions that belong to civil government.

The Jamestown type of Colonies was started largely under the conviction that the highest functions of the English Government was to propagate the gospel abroad.
