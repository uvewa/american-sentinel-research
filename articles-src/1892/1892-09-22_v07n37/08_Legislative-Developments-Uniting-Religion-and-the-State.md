# Legislative Developments Uniting Religion and the State.

Judging from this unanimous decision it becomes a question, what religious enactment by Congress, what religious decree by civil courts, could be unconstitutional?

Further developments must follow such a deliverance from the Supreme Court of the United States. And every progressive religious minority is in readiness to make it possible to push to a universal practice, from such a source. To wish an authority over every act and every thought. That religious minority knows this well. Corresponding developments are to be expected in the schools of the Nation, and thereupon in congressional legislation.

## THE EDUCATIONAL AMENDMENT.

The first bill, having for purpose the union of religion and the State, to receive the attention of Congress, in the committee to which it had been referred, was the proposed Constitutional educational amendment, respecting religion in schools. Its original form was the joint resolution of Senator H. W. Blair, of New Hampshire, May 25, 1888, and as submitted to the Senate by Senator Blair. The first section of this proposed amendment is as follows: "No State shall ever make or maintain any law respecting an establishment of religion or interfering with the free exercise thereof." The proposed amendment, however, contained a provision that the Christian religion be taught in the public schools: "The principles of the Christian religion, including a knowledge of the fundamental and non-sectarian tenets thereof, shall be taught as a part of the educational course." This, by necessity implication, required a test in the professing of the Christian religion as a necessary qualification of a teacher and an examination relating to the position of teacher in the public schools. By this last clause of the proposed amendment the way was opened for Federal and State governments respecting legislation upon religious observance. Had the Blair educational amendment been submitted to the States it would have been an amendment conferring a positive power upon the Statesâ€”it would have been an amendment to the Constitution, and the clause forbidding the application of a religious test as a qualification for a public trust. The original amendment would have been one way to have no religious establishment and no union of church and State. The measure was however finally lost by a very close vote in Congress.
