FIRST—Firstly solving ourselves to be moved by the divine and paramount authority that guarantees the science of religion, we disclaim alike the capacity and the desire to interfere—the promise being that every man's heart is known to God's Royal will and pleasure that none be in any wise molested on account of his religious opinion, that all subjects enjoy the equal and impartial protection of the common law, we sincerely set forth all those who may be in authority under us, that they are most especially enjoined to observe the same rule.

How often in the history of nations "observance" might not only be "disturbed or punished" for evil intent, but imprisoned, for the same reason. What then? Declarations—have these individuals or establishments the exercise of power over the convictions or religious convictions of others, to whom the above provisions pledge "the equal and impartial protection of the common law"?

And how serious is the change in this country. "The Catholic hierarchy should be in authority entire" nor yet, to interfere with "the children's legal beliefs" or worship by religious enactment. I ask, how old is our charter of rights? For instance, then it is obvious that this amended charter must include both House of Colonial Parliaments and Legislatures throughout her wide dominions all of whom has taken the solemn oath of allegiance, including the Privy-Councillors and executive officers of every rank; who are without exception called and summoned "on pain of his highest displeasure," not "to violate the solemn interest." The assertion that any law violated it would be a fatally defective for any purpose whatever. For thirty-four years, since that proclamation was issued, her Majesty Parliament has been faithful to the throne; nor in our Canadian Parliaments to this day charged with any such improper or religious enactment, for which this fundamental right of the British citizen grants to hosts of misguided zealots none but protests against such violation.

Her Majesty's Royal Proclamation was issued on the 1st of November, 1858, by the Governor-General of India, and so is now the charter and bulwark of our religious liberty.

G. W. McCready.

*—Presbyterian Review of Australia.*
