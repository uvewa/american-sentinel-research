A PRIVATE letter gives information that Senator Blair has finally and definitely retired from active work on this matter, and I fail to say why the Sabbath tend to be preserved, when we legislate on the matter. Should the Religious Liberty Association be kept right on this matter, it is not on the side of religious legislation.

BRANCH offices and fifty representatives and fines, lodged against and collected from Sabbath-keepers, amounting in some cases to a total of twenty or five dollars has been lodged, with additional fines of not less than their fines for that violation.

SENATOR PEFFER, of Kansas, in his remarks delivered on this matter, said I fail to say why the Sabbath tend to be preserved, when we legislate on the matter. If it is only the rest of the people, the only legal authority under it. But since the matter has changed, things are not what they were. The mere act of rest and nonattendance at work, shown by a man, whose business is to understand these facts, is not merely a question of rest. The distinction between "legislating to rest" and "legislating for rest" is a distinction that the present national courts of the civil government must settle.

*The* February issue of *The Quarterly Register of Current History* (Detroit: Evening News Association, $1.50 a year), is a work without boasting to the world. It strikes right at the very heart of the subject-matter. From a clear and interesting statement of international affairs, it proceeds to a consideration of related matters of the utmost interest to the general reader. It treats of political parties of Europe and Asia, and, but not least, "Affairs Closer to Home." Among these are included, the presidential discussion, the question of silver coinage, our foreign affairs, the state of trade, finance, and general questions. It also includes political developments in Canada in as clear and interesting a manner.
