THIS last reports the proceedings of the Legislature, in passing the appropriation bill for the Metropolitan Museum of Art.

"Sunday and religion were discussed in the Senate, and there was one of the most remarkable scenes of the session. The occasion was the bill to have the New York City appropriation served annually a year to the Metropolitan Museum of Art, on condition that this Museum be kept open every day of the week, Sundays included. The bill had passed the Assembly without discussion.

"Senator Hunter moved to strike out the Sunday clause. He said that the bill offered a bribe to the Museum authorities to desecrate the Sabbath by opening its doors on the Lord's Day.

"Senator O'Connor said that milk wagons ran on Sunday, and that it was as good for the patron of the Museum to go and see the pictures as to go with nothing to do to get milk and other things to do.

"This is a bid for money at the expense of our religion," said Senator Robertson. "A man goes to Central Park now without making it worse by passing this bill." People can go to the Park and see the animals, eat sandwiches, drink beer, and so on, if they please, without spending a dime and there is no violation on breaking the Sabbath day."

"The Hon. Senator then makes the principal speech in opposition to the bill. "Why break the Sabbath," he said, "and make the modern idea of seventy thousand dollars for breaking down the American Sabbath?" Degradation, to himself with manly faults. Stood by the old faith. Those who go to the Museum on Sundays, but that those who are not there would be glad to go to the Museum some other day, but hangover who might go as well the following Senate presented.

"The bill passed by a vote of seventeen to eight in the Senate and finally the Assembly the concurrent bill. And the Hon. Senator Edwards."
