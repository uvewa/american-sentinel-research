The *Post* fails here to make a correct discrimination as to the part supposed to be played by the different forces and denominations in regard to the World's Fair. The party known as the National League has actually known its demands as to the Sunday closing, and the party has a very strange array of indifference; its requirements is that all shall conform to the customs which all of Christendom are wont to observe, and to allow us to Sunday observance, in the matter of religious opinion upon the Fair. So Sabbatarians who are expressing themselves with earnestness in this matter would feel themselves bound to oppose with equal zeal and persistance any suggestion to enforce civilly the observation of the seventh day. They stand, every one of them, for the principle of the strict non-interference of the civil law in matters of religion, and religion only; that the civil authorities have no right to legislate for or against, neither to force or compel to, in derogation of other religion in obedience or religion to religious observances whatever; that human laws can and only have to do with human relations in the domain of a principle of universal application, bearing equally upon every person, and which is upon him who violates it.

The position of the Sabbatarian is as logically against a Saturday law as it is for the protection of the Sunday observer in his rights. The better way would be to act justly, and let every exhibitor follow the dictates of his own conscience. Let the Sabbatarians exhibit his exhibits on Saturday, but compel no one else to do so; let the observer of another day rest on that day, but compel no one else to do so; let any man rest on whatever day he may choose, on any other day that he may choose, but compel no one to choose one of a different mind to do. This is justice; this is democracy; and this should be the policy that should govern the managers of the Columbian Exposition in 1892.
