Those who are now working so hard in this country for laws to enforce Sunday observance, if they expect to be compelled by law, men will fail to do their whole duty.

Mr. Crafts makes the question in his work, "The Sabbath for Man," on page 263, "Is it not as much a part of the government to legislate for the churches, as such, for the interests of religion or not?"

"Let church conferences and assemblies of every order be free to pass resolutions of their own people, and call upon all men to repent, but it is not a function of civil government."

This is certainly true; but Mr. Crafts knows it is his conviction, and it is as well his religious duty, and Mr. Crafts knows it is more. How then can that which can never been permanently secured in its observance by civil law be made to rest in its extent on the basis of religious obligation? Take the religion out and you have not the fair; it is a religious thing to keep the Sabbath, and to keep it is a religious duty which by the terms of we owe to God. Then when Mr. Crafts “take” up the question and State must, and confuses the thing of the governmental duty out of it, he proposes permanently of Sunday laws and better enforcement of them how is he going to do it? It is God’s day to whom do they owe the duty of keeping it?
