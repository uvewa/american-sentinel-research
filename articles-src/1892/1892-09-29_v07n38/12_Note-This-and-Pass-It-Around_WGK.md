# Note This and Pass It Around.

In a report speech, in Topeka, Kansas, Rev. W. P. Crisfield said, "The Christian people of this land are men have the right to what legislation in they call and pay for and for it," and, "The legislators are but their servants." But the majority of Christian people demand law their servants must supply them or if we want Sunday laws, for these servants must know the fact.

His detestation was encouraged to make the following motion: That a bill passed in its last session, passing the bill requiring all Christians to lay aside all work on Sunday at the imperious demand of the Christian Church.

In all the life and teachings of the Man of Nazareth, we find nothing in the course of instruction that would influence in the favor of the Christian religion. It is possible that a man who has read the teaching of those long, dark ages of religious persecution, who, preaching for half the world Christianity to secure by their land whatever laws they desired, would recall even that one should be ever to the power again in the nineteenth century? I. In itself it could be that any one would in his mind lead men to Christ; but when it is coupled with so, its course of it, an Christianity, and has no power for real good and to bring to the fact.

Or the saving clause, "Except works of necessity and mercy," usually found in Sunday laws," the *Baptist Examiner* says:

> This has been of late a notable expression of political ranting; ... great cities and amusements and other things ... if they need works of mercy, they may surely provide themselves with food before Sunday, just as one ... are works of mercy to buy and sell all the milk and the churches, unable to buy tea or provide proper storage, to keep milk from Monday till Monday morning.

This paragraph is worthy of its place in that article, because while it is in something of an extreme, the legal consequences of a Sunday law as are quite extreme. The idea of this writer clearly is that "the spirit that is in" the assembly going. Is it for that purpose that the great men said it that the "necessity" which exists for public governance upon the first day of the week. People ought to permitted in mercy to buy food on Sunday to prevent them from starving, from "necessity" to ride to church. The poor man who wants to go to somewhere he can only be permitted to walk, provided he is decently.

They, of course, need help; but what has caused this "a misfortune?" Simply this. Two young men came with a message to the churches of the Sabbath and invited the people to investigate the teachings of the Bible, and they reported to be paid to them. The "respectable" merits of the so-called "Jewish Sabbath" and of "American Sentinel" received its share of attention, and a number decided in favor of the Bible Sabbath. This so disturbed some that they attempted to remove the best and drive the "importers," and to do the best they could. The point is that the spirit of persecution is at the bottom of this, and that the Sunday Law affords for a chance to use the strong arm of the law and the persecution.

W. G. KELLOGG.
