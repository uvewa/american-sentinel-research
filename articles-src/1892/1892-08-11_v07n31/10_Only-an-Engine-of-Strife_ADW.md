No instance can be produced of persecuted Christianity attempting to subvert civil government; on the contrary, it is in trusted with a conciliating plan of meekness and peace between all sacred races and things by Jesus Christ. The corruptions of Christianity departed of all the secular under confidence in the moral power of that religion; they bartered purity for power, established among others several positions, and made scandalous truck of all the truths and virtues of religion for the sake of a prison and the love of a free nation. They vented worlds of inexplicable mystery, and those beings people from them who would not interpret their dreams---they cast them into the fire and the dungeon. They stripped the lives and liberties from another portion of their sensible brethren. They gathered around princes with secular power, and entered the barbarous acts of it with the little people---they exacted prisons to hate, persecuted, banish, and bind; their subjects for matters of conscience---they thought by edicts to benefit their nation, kings above law, and themselves above kings. To their combined it is owing that more than an engine of State.---*Robinson. 1633.*

might find out what kind of a tree temperance grows on, and then they would give up their effort to compel a corrupt tree to bear spiritual fruit. And if the power that is consumed in the effort to influence legislation, and secure the aid of the civil power to force the gospel upon men, were put forth in proclaiming the gospel as God directs, the Holy Spirit would have free course. If they would trust that they would not need to be ashamed of the gospel of Christ, for it is the power of God unto salvation. Paul cared nothing about those who were "having a form of godliness, but denying the power thereof;" he said, "from such turn away." A gospel which denies the power of God by distrusting the civil power to establish its claims, we have no need of. Its adoption only shows that as a movement of its age it will never succeed. Its supporters may succeed in securing control of the civil power; they may succeed in persecuting to the death every one who will not bow down and give up their conscience; but their crown of religious reformer, but they will not succeed in converting one soul; or in promoting true temperance.

Yet, the prohibition movement, on the other and in view than to add temperance, must be clearly drawn; and not on the side of error, because the State has no right to attempt any such function as prescribing to men of faith. One belongs to the gospel; and, second, because it is supposed to be forced, since by powers can have in the protection of peaceable citizens whether the individual or of churches. This is on the principles that the Government should prohibit that which experience, long established usage, and impartial judgment on the most invariable cause of crime. The prohibition of the manufacture and sale of intoxicating liquor has more relation to temperance than it has to Christianity, or to religion. And the object of government in the protection of each citizen, against the bad conduct of others.

A. DELOS WESTCOTT.
