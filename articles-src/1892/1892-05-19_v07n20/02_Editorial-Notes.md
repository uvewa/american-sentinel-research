THE Parkhurst episode is still attracting a good deal of attention in this city. The question is not when the man whose home contained the beauty show, has been graded in the community of the best people of as the world goes, she is discovering of no account whatever.

But the fact of the guilt of this wicked and depraved woman, does not justify a minister of the gospel in invading the best powers to use him as a purpose as enticing women to indecent acts in order that he may become a complainant witness against them. The grand commission to its creation, and if so it does not follow that the minister has no authority to play the detective.

*Crime itself mildly not only with the erring, but with the abandoned.* To the woman taken in adultery he said, "Neither do I condemn thee; go ye, and sin no more." And why did our Lord not condemn her in adultery? Let any honest mind answer: "For God sent not his Son into the world to condemn the world, but that the world through him might be saved."

CHRIST did not excuse sin; he granted no indulgences to sinners. The sin and the sinner could at one and the same time be both forgiven and destroyed; but the law could not at once be justified. Even when appeared to be settle a civil difference, by declining to act as a civil judge, he answered, "Who made me a divider and a judge over you?" Such work was foreign to Christ's mission, and as he is "no greater than his lord," should not the minister of Christ go and do likewise?

AGAIN, should a Christian ever go where he can not proclaim himself a Christian Society? a minister ever go where he must deny his religion? That is a question for ministers to answer then. But this is just what Dr. Parkhurst's ministers went out to do. They went where their very work led them to do. As related by Mrs. John May, a colored woman of New Jersey, in the *Mail and Express* of Monday, May 3, she asked the Doctor this question, and how Dr. Parkhurst replied that it was to expose recognition in the parlours of New York, as a man of the highest culture and character. Dr. Parkhurst answered that he had been told a thing by a judge or minister, and replied, "I am an officer and acting of justice." There is no ecclesiastic or civil law on the earth which requires a denial of his sacred office, and the last property to be done by a minister of the Lord ought to be that.
