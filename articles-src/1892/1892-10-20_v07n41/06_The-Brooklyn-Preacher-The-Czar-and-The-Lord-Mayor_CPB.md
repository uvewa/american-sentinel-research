# The Brooklyn Preacher, The Czar, and The Lord Mayor.

REV. DR. TALMAGE has returned to his work. He is not a happy and contented man. He went on a mission of charity in which the high hand had been taken out of its former as to what the left hand was doing. As a matter of course, therefore, a most delightful reciprocity of feeling was the not consequence, and Dr. Talmage profited much by the trip. His big heart has much crushed. He, consequently, comes back to Brooklyn with a well filled and courtly language of compliments still ringing in his ears. He has discovered new perceptions and un-American views, with which he has become infected, have not escaped both guarnation and the custom house officer. Immediately upon arriving at Brooklyn does the Doctor proceed to unpack his foreign goods and give them out liberally to the American public through the reporters. The newspaper of his substitution of Russian royalty is harmless, though amusing, and indicates a state of mind and a course of whose practice it should be to be no respecter of persons.

The Czar, I think, is one of the grandest of men, and he is one of the best men in the world. The Emperor, too, is one of the noblest characters of his kind. He also is doing the best he can for his people. The Emperor, too, is one of the noblest characters; his face beaming with kindness and benevolence.

It need be said of an aged historical ecclesiastic, to whom respect is given, of note whose lectures were once much in vogue, a man of culture, and a citizen of New York, that his lectures upon Queen Elizabeth, and his description of certain elements as being "retrospectively synonymous" of the royal legitimacy. Dr. Talmage has spoken very recklessly of his language between the world and spirit of mind of a mind overcome with sweet sycophancy, and courtly ritual, and royal finery, until he has become enamored of its royalty. But it was the utterances and reflections of the Brooklyn divine on the subject of religion and government, which inspired the delightful old lecturer, of which have their significance in the question which takes all its character from the pulpit.

Not only his conclusions, taken collectively, and many, from his personality, become somewhat impressive and therefore significant. The evidences are that the discrimination of the renowned preacher, not only is he afflicted with this overwhelming sycophancy; not only has he been deeply impressed with the royal prerogatives of king, and of king, but he has been wined and dined and banqueted and honored by the bishops and canons, and he has been brought to the point of responding to the toast of "Religion in America." These are the facts.
