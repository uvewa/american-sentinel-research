The following extracts from the *News Letter* give the situation. In the first page of the letter, under the heading of "Somerville's first Experience in Puritanical Persecution," the paper says:

Last Sunday our citizens had their first experience of . . . Puritanical persecution, in compliance with the order issued by the Chief of Police of Somerville, two policemen visited all the stores on the avenue. . . . Those which they found open for business were notified to close. . . . All complied with the order, and within half an hour the avenue was without an open store . . . and remained so until Sunday. The druggist was also required to close at once. . . . The cause for the issue of this order was the complaint of a person not an account of the Blue Laws, and of a similar character.

While these little grievances may be considered serious enough by those having to endure them; the enforcement of these statutes will work vastly more serious results for the whole community, as well as to individuals, should other cases come or be driven to the wall. Not mere policy and enforcement of law, it is unjust and clearly means not a matter of policy and right, it, but principle is involved. Once let the spirit of bigotry get hold of this city of law and persecution is sure to follow. The truth of this assertion is attested in every land. Religion and morality cannot be forced upon the people by legislative enactments.

A whole world of truth is wrapped up in that one sentence. And the writer of it was made fair and square, and we will wanton to see any person who can ever be convinced after he has considered and told this than righteousness.

It would be an infinitely more just and humane law that every one shall be hungry for a day, whenever the community as hungry the other day, than to pass a law that they shall be full of food. . . . That law would be just as successful as the other; both would be equally futile. But the law concerning the State slipping into just such absurdities, concerning matters of this kind, that State and Church forever separate; . . . a simple fact of her sister States, and seen her sisters of other States take warning, and beware of Arkansas, Tennessee and Maryland. Let other States take warning, and beware of the Puritanical bigotry and hatred. Only failure awaits such efforts before it kills the tender growth.
