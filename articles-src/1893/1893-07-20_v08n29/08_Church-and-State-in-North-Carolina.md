If the Revolution really began in North Carolina, as the natives of that State content, it was because the people were even induced by a desire for religious freedom than by the wish for political independence. The first State Constitution, adopted by the Provincial Congress at Halifax in November, 1776, guarantees to every citizen the right of men to worship Almighty God according to the dictates of his own conscience. It was further laid down that there should be no establishment of any one religious church or denomination in the State of North Carolina in preference to any other. Neither should any person, on any pretence, be compelled to attend any place of worship contrary to his own faith and judgment; nor be obliged to pay for the purchase of any globe of the holding of any house of worship, or the maintenance of any minister; that not the first, he believed right, or had voluntarily and without coercion joined. In this matter, certainly, all persons should be at liberty according to their own understanding. These declarations invited the downfall of the Established Church. It only remained for the laws of the new State to be brought into conformity with the Constitution. Marriage was put on a proper footing in 1778, by a law giving the privileges of performing the ceremony to all ministers alike.
