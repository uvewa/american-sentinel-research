THE *Soldier,* a paper published at Columbus, Ga., in the interest of ex-Confederate soldiers, has, in the issue of June 1, 1893, about a month, half-filled of explanatory and so-called National Reform as it may set on one page.

The writer of the article, an advocate of the American Sabbath Union, has a long article entitled "The Lord's Day, Which?" in which he takes up the Sabbath question and answers the claims held by the Seventh-day Baptists and Seventh-day Adventists.

He says that while they "most of the time doctrines designated as evangelical, and are a wealthy class of citizens;" yet "they have placed themselves in antagonism to the movement for preserving the safeguards which new protect Sunday." And this opposition on the part of the leaders has assumed a violence that partakes of its malice; and a pride of opinion that will be satisfied with nothing short of uprooting the existing institution and creed of Christendom. "They confess they are friends and friends of the Bible great and before legislation." "They hold that while the Government has only Bible Sabbath, every one should be left perfectly free, so far as civil law is concerned, to observe any day, or no day at all, according to his own pleasure or his own sense of duty to God. On this point their influence and arguments are necessarily on the side of those who would remove the safeguards which protect the day."
