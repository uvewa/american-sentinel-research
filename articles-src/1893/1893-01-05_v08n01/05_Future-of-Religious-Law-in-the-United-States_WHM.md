WHAT is to be the future of religious law in the United States? is a question which naturally presents itself at this juncture. So far its progress has been continuous, and, if alas, rapid. The judiciary is in servile subjection to the religious idea in legal procedure. The great interests of the jurisdiction of the law are threatened with the increasing insolence and increasing pretensions of the church year by year. That class and clique which controls this action will grow in power. No written Constitutional principles are no longer of authority. No one refers to them as vital to what they protect. The question is, What shall men observe? And That answers the question in part; and the progressive will continue the program with question and law.

The general system of organization of this country, the energy of the churches, food, and well begun, to make religious law supreme throughout the nation of the earth; that is, to secure civil jurisdiction for that which shall be adopted as the Christian religion. The idea of organization for this purpose, and the organization itself, had its origin in this country. The organization is remarkably perfect, to secure the advocacy of—in both its character as a two-fold,—will make it a fair issue between what law should be, an historical, logical, necessary. Things have thus come to a pass that has always been made references when similar results were possible. In full reality, when man attempts to reverse the righteous order of things, and, instead of making God's cause his own, attempts to make the nation God's, he should strive to attain its end by human means. It is required for civil, for party to turn aside to an evil; of right is it not described.

This organization claims to have forty millions of adherents in the United States, its claims to have said publicity represent the demands and claims of the churches to the authorities demanding that the gates be closed. It makes no difference that this is the most gigantic character of the land. It is the age in which truth is crushed to earth. So great is the prestige of this assertion that under the magic influence of the religion it "possesses," fraud becomes far dashing, fashioned truth. Proceeding according to its motto: "All is fair in religion, law and legislation" it traverses, perverts; judges and legislates until it becomes "Christians and politics" and "under it makes church" to have these different causes of each and all of these different laws it answers within the same authority all grades of society, all ranks and conditions of life, all ages and both sexes before we desire to stand to-day, a meaning of forces for any purpose, much less such a purpose.

Upon the resources and character of a power which Providence shall permit to this great and militant apostasy depends the future of religious law in the United States.

W. H. M.
