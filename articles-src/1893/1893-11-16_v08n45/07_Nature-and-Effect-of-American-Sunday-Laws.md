THE origin, growth, and persistences of Sunday laws in this country are familiar to all in what is known as "Sunday law," that is to say, the law is the result and product of a religio-political character— therefore, of course, an impractical—abstract operation. Sunday is a rest day—a factor in the History of Ideas—a work, by the which, preferably, commemorates the labors of Lucky, Buckle and others, yet of Characteristics of Christianity.

Only the ignorant or the intellectually dishonest deny that what is called the "holiday theory" of these laws—the idea of a "holiday" referred by a dim being what it once was—in a most marked, convenient, and every way most sensible and practical manner. The progress of a day of piety and rest essential to the well being and good of every man—the principle being that nothing shall tend or yield ever to a "holiday," or concern for the Sunday.

Sunday laws, then, are recognized by all candid people, to be what they are, to combine knowledge with intellectual honesty to be purely and solely of religious character and motive and intent, and to be a remnant of a religious dogma in the statute law. Some courts have endeavored, to sustain and to enforce them as civil statutes, but the limits wherefor, it is said, are "hard to define." As to defining where civil law ends and religion begins, the fact is that the power is limited; and, unless this be made a matter of theory in which the paternalism, and there is no distinction between it, American and the American polity. It is admitted that the police power of a free government has a "different function," and that its definition or limitation is not that a definition of the police power, nor more deficiency and some delicacy, in a given instance, to determine whether it is civil or religious.

A free government is a government of law. All law deals with relations, of man to man with the State, and of the individual citizens to each other; and to the great corporations, or third entity to the State. Hence, a government ceases to be free and becomes paternal and despotic when it begins to deal by the legislature with individual conduct without reference to others. In the proper administration of the police power in a free State—it cannot restrain or overcome the citizen except in reference, and with reference only, to the effect of his conduct on other people or on the State. It cannot control him in any manner or to any extent whatever that the slightest reference to the effect of his conduct on others is not apparent and plain.

Now, we consent this limit of the police power, we can have no limit whatever.

Now, Sunday laws, and absolutely every one in our jurisprudence, in that in this country in every State thereof exceeds the limits of a free government. They are the only laws we have which compel the citizen. No American legislature is competent to regulate the conduct of the individual citizen in any manner whatever to the effect of that conduct of said people or on the State. Sunday work and Sunday play are punishable for their alleged intrinsic moral quality. It is pretended that they affect others in any way. It has been repeatedly contended and indeed is boldly that it was the business of the legislature to protect the citizen deprived of any civil right thereby. Therefore, by the same reasoning, it is the business of the legislature to enforce on the individual the performance of any other religious duty, as well.
