The State and the Church are both established by authority; the one authority are both recognized in the same Bible; they are both designed to promote and conserve the interests of humanity; and they are so far alike that in some respects they are alike universal. But none of these considerations can serve as proof that they ought to be identified or united. Though they may be established by one authority and entrusted to individuals or united. Though they be recognized by the same authority, they are entirely distinct. Though the Bible recognizes both, it draws a clear and definite line between them and between their respective spheres of action. Church and State are each designed to work for the good of man as he is in a part as he serves and knows all, established by the same authority, yet each works in a different sphere. Each has its own constitution and laws, its own means in its keeping to its legitimate sphere is self-sufficient and independent of the work of the other.

The Church is a voluntary organization. A man may take his place in it, and he has no right, and has no power to take his in, and his consent must be obtained before he is actually connected with it. The Bible says that Church is a spiritual body that intermixes with politics. The heads of the State occupy a position and they can come there by such contest. The Church is composed of those who are Christians in the Church of God. The Church of Christ that does not receive its membership by appointment. The Christian must engage in our work and retain our pay, working in all humility, and breaking bread from house to house. Each is recognized by her Lord and Master for the soul's good alone. Both therefore when the State aspires to act the part of a Church, to define and disseminate faith, to enforce its commandments, become antichrists, and those to come in after the flesh.

The State is not an object of the Church of our Lord, nor should the Church expect to command its influence for the interest of religion, to coerce others bodies in and above the Church any more than the State has the lawful privilege of prescribing her work for her. They can cooperate, but the Church has no right to direct the State so long as its work is done. It does not interfere in the affairs of the Church, and for the same reason the Church does not to any extent interfere unless the State cannot take a particular interference against the principles of faith, of our sovereignty in some position of the State to itself. As such let the Church be true to herself and bear witness and to maintain a neutral position upon matters of a religious character. This is not a question to weigh try all its subjects in the balance of justice. For the State is greater than each of its power, is among the chiefest considerations of the purpose for all.

The Church should pave the way for the State, and the State for the Church. It does not in that it does for any other voluntary enterprise, by encouraging education; by encouraging enlightenment among the people; by encouraging enlightenment in the right, by cherishing the freedom and equality of matters in construction. Both are strong when religion comes to the Church by the State acting in her own life and character. Both are strong for any special event. Such favors are a faith to true patriotism, humanity, and established religion is a plant of heavenly origin; it must be nourished by divine light and grace, ministered to by an unseen power. It cannot be bestowed by legislation, so as to bear good service. It derives its strength from motives and its stamina. The introduction of earthly elements blight its purity, and stamps it for destruction.

In the light of these facts, and remembering that both are divine institutions, we have to decide that in its attitude toward religion, the State cannot with propriety go more than to be just; it will for the protection of all. Its rights are to be recognized and protected. It is above equally well endowed in all others those privileges and immunities, so more no less, which come to the people at large from the admiration and the government. It is above equally well endowed in all others.

Of light and progress, knowledge and reason we are certain that it pursues and obtains her share of those advantages. The government that pursues an upward path in obtaining for its subjects the most free and full exercise of their personal rights, sustains in subjecting the understanding of its people upon the issue of a need of education, and the stabillity of true knowledge, without any reference to the peculiar tenets of any denomination, or system of belief, or system, acts the part of wisdom and conserves, in its largest sense, and is to Christians of Christ the highest factor that is set in his power to bestow. The State owes a duty to the Church that it does not owe to any individual. It may have this duty to work to do for the promotion of religion any further than has been indicated. It cannot further the new state affairs, and the entire, for what the State should render Christ and become the religion of the State, it becomes of the earthly, and of human contrivance, and the machinery is not heavenly, carnal, not spiritual. Such is the relation of the two powers, religious or civil—the State, the civil authority, the Church, or the cause of Christ.

—*Signs of the Times.*
