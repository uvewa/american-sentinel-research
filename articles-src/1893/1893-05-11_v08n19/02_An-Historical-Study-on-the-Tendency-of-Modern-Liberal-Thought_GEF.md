In the second and third Christian centuries, the world of philosophical and theological thought and feeling had been divided and subdivided into contending factions. Among the more prominent of these were the Academics, the Stoics and the Platonics; each with a different view of God, and each having a different solution for the great problem of life. Among the Jews, there were the Pharisees, the Sadducees, the Essenes, and the believers in Jesus.

Christianity itself, so recently from the hands of its Founder and his apostles, had already bedecked its robe in the rich and gaudy ornaments of factionalism; cling to the simple teaching of Christ and the apostles, their followers were divided into Christians, the Nazarenes, or the Ebionites, as they were afterward called, and the Gnostics already subdivided into several sects, till of these combining Christianity with oriental mysticism. Then, there were a hundred different forms of pagan worship, originating in as many different nations; yet composed of so many different elements that are united by flocking to the Pantheon to find place in each and every system of philosophy asserted itself with more or less, dogmatic force, and proclaimed itself in possession of the ultimate truth.

What wonder that under such conditions of matters, there should arise a set of good, earnest men, who looked upon this wild scramble for philosophical and religious supremacy as a disgrace, the very denial of the broad spirit, which obliged every one to swear allegiance to the dictates of his master?

Such were the Eclectics, who did not believe that any one creed was broad enough to hold all the truth, but rather that the truth lay dispersed in scattered fragments among all the systems of belief and philosophy. As their master taught, they "professed to simply follow truth," gathering up what was accorded with it wherever it might be found; that practically they would avoid sectarianism and all the warring parties, and that if all men of the world have come together on an Eclecticism on a broad international basis of religion, what a magnificent opportunity for the formation of a new creed. However, in the early days, men let us welcome each member more openly, and these Eclectic philosophers borrowed one from another, exchanging platitudes. "This philosophy was adopted by each of the learned in Alexandria, so that the Eclectics had a special home. It will retain the name, the garb, and the language of every school of philosophy, but the one creed soon proved too narrow for these men of liberality; and it was merged one into another, which for broad and comprehensive liberality, could not possibly be surpassed by this genius of a modern, even though he ran ahead of himself in dignifying and unifying a whole international congress of religions.
