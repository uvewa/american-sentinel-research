THE Christian doctrine of justification is, that it is by faith alone, with the faith itself the gift of God, so that it is wholly of the Lord in a free gift.

"WHATSOEVER is not of faith is sin." Rom. 14:23. Conversely, whatsoever *is* of faith is righteousness. Consequently righteousness is of faith only. And the faith being the gift of God the righteousness of faith is inevitably the righteousness of God. See Rom. 3:21; Phil. 3:9.

It is not by faith *and* works; it is by faith *which* works. The faith, being the gift of God, is a divine thing, bearing in it the divine which conveys to every sinner who will receive it, the righteousness of God in that true covering of that past; and it is also the divine power to keep the justified one in the way of righteousness.

For in the Christ of God the righteousness of God revealed from faith to faith, as it is written, The justification of faith. Hab. 2:4; Rom. 1:17. "And in Christ Jesus neither circumcision availeth any thing, nor uncircumcision; but faith which worketh by love." Gal. 5:6. If Abraham believed God and if (his faith) was counted unto him for righteousness, not reckoned of grace, but of debt. But to him that worketh not, but believeth on him that justifieth the ungodly, his faith is counted for righteousness. Rom. 4:3-5.

The true Protestant doctrine of justification is just this Christian doctrine of justification. But there is now on earth the Catholic doctrine of justification which is directly the opposite of this.

THAT it may be seen how certainly this is so, we give the Catholic statement of the case. In telling what was done in this respect, at the time of the "so-called" Reformation, the statement is as follows:—

> To make up for the *rejection* [of the Catholic sacraments] and enable each individual to prescribe for himself, and *procure for himself* [pardon of sin, and divine grace, independently of the priests and of the sacraments, they invented an article of faith—*justification by faith alone*—unknown to the whole Christian Church before Luther—denied by the word of God, and still rejected by all the Eastern churches . . . . By adopting this principle of justification] which the followers of Luther returned to the heathen each individual can procure this justification for himself, independently of priests and sacraments.—*Catholicity* p. 96.

> They have framed a new dogma, not to be found in any of the creeds, or in the council of any age and council. I mean *the new dogma of justifying faith alone*, on which, as on a pillar, the whole building of the Reformation rests.—*Id.* p. 80.

> By adding the word alone, Protestants gratuitously deny works, works of obedience or of consent, and good moral acts whatever, as *means of apprehending or reaching towards Christ*—justifying faith alone.—*Catholicity* p. 93.

> Having invented a thing which he called justifying faith, to be a *sufficient substitute* for all his above named religious works; an invention which took of every responsibility from one about himself and laid it on the shoulders of *Jesus Christ.*—*Catholicity* p. 87.

> To the minds now a view of being justified, as they [Protestants] say, the giving a power to the sinner to seize from his own fireside! Come to us, they say, you remain in *our* body; and a firm trust, you remain in us for all forgiveness.

> Through faith alone in his promises, they tell you that he not only imputes, but applies to the sinner Christ's redemption and his justice [righteousness]; imputation—that by this faith alone Jesus is with you; is yours, that his pardon your sins; and all his wisdom and preparation without saying or doing on your part; in fact that this imaginary *faith alone* may be in all sin; that which Catholic *regain, and licences from restraint* with each of you from Jesus all that *trust alone confers, pardon, mercy, redemption, and a right to heaven, to every one.*—*Catholicity* p. 80, 87. And the italics are all in the original.

Bear in mind that this is the Catholic Church's statement of the Protestant doctrine of justification. And bear in mind that the Catholic Church thus plainly claims that this doctrine was "never known to the Church of God," "is not to be found in any of the creeds or the council of *any* general council," and that "it is still *unregarded* by Roman Catholics throughout the world."

Very good. That is correct. No true Protestant could ask for any better statement of the case. And this Protestant doctrine of justification, which was so emphatically repudiated and opposed by Catholicism—this doctrine is the Christian doctrine of justification, as every one who has ever read the Bible for himself. Consequently no better evidence is needed to show that the Catholic doctrine of justification is certainly anti-christian.

For, mark it, the "faith" laid by the Catholic Church is only "the faith of obedience."—*Catholicity* p. 83, 84.

THIS is "the faith" of the Catholic Church. And this "faith" which the Church holds, is of itself wholly human; and being human, makes the creed, the sacraments the test, and the merit, and the whole ritual of the church, to enforce that obedience in which all "faith" consists; "faith" in this sense being nothing other than obedience to the Church.
