HISTORY is not an artificial grouping of isolated facts. "No man liveth unto himself;" and no fact is an isolated event in life. Equally true is it that no fact in history is isolated. Every student of history asserts, says that there is a relation between the facts of one life and the conditions of time, much more is there an organic relationship, existing between the facts of history. One stands related to events previously, which have caused it, and to events following after, of which it is the cause. Thus, it is a poor student of history who sees in the battle of Waterloo, only the battle of Waterloo, its date, the forces engaged, numbers killed, and the result; all of this is a great moral wounded, the name of the genius in command, and unless his inquiry leads far afield. He will be a student in a true sense, which will accept a system of questions. Why did this battle occur? What was its consequence for future?

In no branch of investigation is the question "Why?" more important, and "What follows?" more essential, than in history. It calls for something more than to search out the essence of relation. It may well also be made the basis from which to view the import of the "Move, move, follow up, follow up," marking the march of events. Some one has said that history is an interaction of causes and effects, each in turn reacting upon one another. And the success of the student of history depends on his ability to interpret a fact in its relation to other facts, and its bearing upon the future.
