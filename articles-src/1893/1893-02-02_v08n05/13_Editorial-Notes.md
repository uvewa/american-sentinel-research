NEW YORK, FEBRUARY 2, 1893.

------

ANY one receiving the AMERICAN SENTINEL without having ordered it, may know that it is sent to them by some friend. Those who have not ordered the SENTINEL need not fear that they will be asked to pay for it, as no paper whatever will proceed from this house that is not paid for in advance by somebody else or by themselves they take it from the post-office.

------

READ the article on page 34, "Facts vs. Fictions." It is hard to find two such facts. "Facts are stubborn things;" true the ones given in this article are not rare, but a good many people have forgotten them or overlooked them entirely.

------

THE *Adventist* seems soon to be tried in Paris, Henry County, Tenn., will be reported for the SENTINEL by one of its editors who is on the ground for the purpose. These reports will be valuable, and the events of the trial will supply facts relative to the Tennessee persecution, not only for us own reading, but to put into the hands of others. We can not foretell just what members the report will appear, or in how many, but the papers will be furnished at the rate of two cents per copy.

------

BOTH houses of the Illinois legislature recently passed a resolution asking Congress to repeal the Sunday-closing provision in the World's Fair bill, whereupon the citizens of Springfield called a mass meeting of the people of that city to protest against the action of the legislators. The meeting was extensively advertised, but only two or a hundred persons were present. Nevertheless, the resolutions set forth that "the citizens of Springfield assembled" as "in the mass-meeting do most respectfully yet earnestly protest."

------

The Sabbath *Outlook*, a Seventh-day Baptist monthly, published at Alfred Centre, this State, is very outspoken in its opposition to Sunday legislation. We have often quoted from it in the SENTINEL. But this is not a question of man's best or of right. Sunday laws would be no better than they now are if there was not a single Sabbatarian to raise his voice against them. It is all the same so far as the protest is concerned. It would still be true that they are wrong in principle.

Is not this plan being worked on now on a much larger scale? And is not comparatively few people, under the leadership of the Sunday preachers, making Congress think things that they are. Does the United States is in favor of Sunday closing?

------

The *Mail and Express* says "the World's Fair Directors are committed to Sunday closing. By every consideration of honor and good faith they are bound to adhere to it." But they are bound also by the of the Congressional contribution of the management of the Columbian Exhibit to provide for the expenses of the celebration. The condition was that the Fair should be closed on Sunday. They accepted the congressional grant under these conditions, and by consequence they are bound by both moral and legal obligations to either surrender the grant or to acknowledge and enforce the condition.

Why has Congress not taken Sunday closing? It has been adopted so Congress had power to close the Fair has it not also power to open it? If it had power to impose the Sunday-closing condition has it not at least an equal right to remove the direction from that condition? Or is it only the Sunday advocates who have a right to ask of Congress?
