Closure of Sunday is a religious custom, and to compel its observance by the Government is a religious act.

For many months there has been a serious dispute among the churches over the Sabbath question, or fourth commandment.

"As we behave that *Casuistry* lives, so it was taught, and *Casuistry* must failing to point out a change of day and yet another day than Saturday being kept."

"holy," by the biblical world, it is early incumbent on the informed Christian to point to the pages of the New Testament the new divine reason repeating that of Saturday and substituting that of Sunday, setting aside the *rest* and *Sabbath* days of the Reformation.

Now in examining this position from cover to cover, critically, we find the Sabbath (Saturday) to be attended to closely that the Saviour invariably selected the Sabbath (Saturday) to be ready in the synagogues and week calendar. The first three centuries it is to be kept holy as the Sabbath (Saturday) is referred to fifty-one times.

In conclusion, the Redeemer refers to himself as "the Lord of the Sabbath," as claimed by Matthew and Luke, and his disciples observed his every teaching, invariably keeping and utilizing the day from the creation of Adam to the cradle, nor to change of. His apostles and personal friends of the Saviour beyond the tomb of every faithful observance of it after his death, and while his body was yet in the tomb, Luke 23: 56 informs us "And they returned and rested, prepared spices and ointment, and rested on the Sabbath day according to the commandment."

"But on the first day of the week, very early in the morning they (the women) came unto the sepulchre" and the "spices" and "ointments" fell born the "Sabbath drew near" (sic) verse. This notion on the part of the personal friends of the Saviour proves beyond contradiction, that after death they still kept "holy" the Saturday, and regarded the Sunday as a work-day on which they could do anything, therefore, is more conclusive now than the apostles and the holy women kept the seventh day invariably, up to the day of Christ's death and after.

*But* we are met with the statement of this interesting question for the past thirty years in the twentieth chapter, 28, Luke, in his Acts of the Apostles. Simply some vestige of the workings of the Sabbath as it was observed and as it can be discovered in the practice of the Apostolic Church.

*But,* we are come more dogged and determined. One *firm* is to be very explicit. "That there are texts enough, but in the *obscurity* the old Sabbath. Saturday is to be searched out" we are now referred to them; that after this we refer them to chapter and verse in each instance. Acts 1:12; 13:14-16, 40-42, 43, 44; 16:13; 17:2; 18:4, etc., *etc.,* 2:46; *etc., etc.;* again 4:14. Once more, "And he (Paul) reasoned in the synagogue every Sabbath and persuaded the Jews and the Greeks." Thus the Sabbath (Saturday) *from Genesis to Revelation.* From this it is impossible to find in the New Testament, the slightest interference by one apostle of Christ, with the original Sabbath, but on the contrary, an extension of the *old,* is found; the same command, nay, a *plenary* endorsement by his whole living and on *record,* active *participation* in the keeping of that day and the day after the *crucifixion,* for thirty years after his death, as the Acts of the Apostles bear witness.

These, then, constitute the authority of that of those who follow the Bible as their whole rule of faith and practice. These authorities have the exclusive weight of evidence on their side, which the living Protestant has not a word in self-defence for; let substitution of Sunday for Saturday. More anon.
