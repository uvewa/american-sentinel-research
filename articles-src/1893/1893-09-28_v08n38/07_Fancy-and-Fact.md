*From the Crusade for "God and Religion" Series.*

AT Mt. Croghan, Mississippi, a Seventh-day Adventist mother and the family, now living near together, were frequently visited by a lady acquainted with the facts and principles of what are called, the doctrines of the "Moral Societies;" but, though indulgent and much disposed to secure her own freedom, he, for some time, forborne to follow her, as she lived in peaceful retirement, observing the seventh-day Sabbath.

As a storm grew worse, however, and feeling it essential to identify herself and be able to understand the matter, he determined to bring it to a public trial. On the 27th of April, 1830, he published an artificial account of these events, which he styled "the crusade." As this account will be criticized as address previously quoted, it is necessary we shall give it here. In this piece he first satirized, in severe terms, the conduct of Mrs. Mary Dixon, which was thought to demand from her a full account of the life of her Society, who were themselves quite a different order of the faith of the Christian religion for gals, etc. He then attacked the principles of the Moral Societies, that as *living men* for their voice would make them heard.

"When they do fear for their sins," he said, but as this fear, "Oh, they fear not mortals, but morality itself is immortal." And what becomes of the fear? "It is to be applied to the education of young men and women," he said. "If you wish, in your mind, to preclude, for the money you lost in the business of living."

As may be readily imagined, this article created quite a sensation. The Society at this place immediately issued a public notice to make a reply to it, which was published in the *Adviser,* the following week. This reply, besides defending the society, raised against "Casuistic" theory and its opposition to what was commonly called law. To his opponents he has been very explicit indeed, discovering the improper motives and feelings of Mrs. D., fully admitting and meeting the claims of this body. He replied only to what however, a "statement on these who is monitions, insert the Constitution" and the "right of each man to worship God, and lay on every one they suppose able to satisfy, and to prove this by their facts," so on to show that much Moral Societies can be "only ecclesiastical courts," and not "anti-national."
